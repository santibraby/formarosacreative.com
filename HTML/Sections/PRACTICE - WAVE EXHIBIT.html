Off a Soho street, the ground turns to pasture - green underfoot, a horizon at eye level.

Wave Exhibit by Forma Rosa Creative is an immersive environment designed to host the Wave Column in its many variations, a ceramic fixture by Forma Rosa Studio.

The spatial concept is a nod to the picturesque movement at gallery scale - an antithesis to the contemporary technological reform, and a return to our pastoral heritage. Meditative and gritty, both beautiful yet sublime, real dirt installed in the window display pulls you in with an added sense, and ceramic sculptures that read as both artifact and organism, stand tall like tree trunks and glow as if alive.

A continuous podium wraps the room's perimeter, and the backing undulates in a wave form echoing the column's own feminine silhouette. The hand-applied tadelakt, pigmented from deep green into sandy beige then into dark blue, runs from the ground all the way up, covering the podiums, walls and ceilings - signaling pasture, dry earth, dusk sky, and centering your view to the horizon.

Mirrored walls kaleidoscope, expanding your view into what Jay Appleton calls prospect: an unbroken sightline deep into the distance.

IN CONFIGURATION

The Wave Column

Three Wave Columns in various modular configurations anchor the gallery, while three more pull visitors in from the window display. Pendants and a standing lamp showcase the different functions of the collection, built around a ceramic module.

Real dirt mixed with fall leaves, the scent of a walk in the park in the fall, is held in a plexiglass case at the window, as if the landscape has been sliced, and we can see the layers just beneath our feet. Tadelakt is hand-applied to plywood, all the corners sanded smooth, giving a rich and full texture for the window display set, and interior scenography.

A scattering of spotlight minis brings another dimension to the evening window display: a memory of stars at dusk over a campsite in the San Juan Islands, carried indoors.

INTERVIEW

In Conversation

Fast-forward 20 years. The exhibit is no longer new - it has lived. Why does this work still matter?

I hope that by bringing natural aesthetics back into contemporary culture one design at a time, we will be reminded as a species where we come from, and that we will place value back into our heritage out on the savannah, we will let time slows down, we will allow ourselves to be more present, and we will continue to listen to the natural environments around us and learn from them, as we have for millennium.

The thing about natural aesthetics is that they are the backbone for all design, this is not something that is a trend and will go away, each step of the way we are just slowly progressing towards a unification with our natural environments. Everything we do as a species is rooted in our collective experience in nature. The clothes we wear, the way we interact with architecture, the tools and objects we use in day to day life. Our airplanes are symbols crafted to mimic birds.

The complexity of natural phenomena, like the crystal glazework on a Forma Rosa lamp arising from a chemical reaction of minerals when kiln-fired, are not something we try to control, but that we harness and display. As a designer I act as the messenger, bringing visitors into the natural worlds that I have experienced in my memories.

When does the design stop being a project and become something human?

The forms and shapes, the textures and patterns, they feel familiar because they are. They are me, they are you, they are all of us. They are you as a child looking up at the stars for the first time outside of the city. They are the first time you visited a cornfield and saw the dark green stalks, beige tips, and deep blue sky above, and the smell of manure surrounded you. They are that one time you walked through the forest in the Pacific Northwest and 90% of your field of view was deep green - the ferns, the pine trees, the moss growing on absolutely everything, even the lake far in the distance was emerald and not even the sky was showing through the thick canopy.

Fast forward 20 years and this might just be a memory of a long lost culture. But I don't think it will be. I think we are heading backwards, I think the isolation from nature in cities, is drawing people back out, and this past will once again become more and more our present. I hope we can share these experiences with those around us, and show how healing these spaces can be.

Who is the person moving through this space - and what do they feel?

While there has been pushback in recent years towards temporary exhibitions and experiential activations, because of sustainability and how wasteful a temporary built environment can be, I really believe environmental design is one of the only architectural scale typologies that can be truly immersive. Because of this, the space acts as a portal, transporting the users into a new world, where they can connect with aesthetics and concepts at a tangible and fully sensory level. I want the users to feel as if they are remembering a moment from their childhood, a moment of nostalgia but also a moment of alienation - as you might feel walking a new trail by yourself in the late fall. I've been scared walking alone in the woods, I've been in awe, I've been cold, I've been out of breath and exhausted, I've also felt at peace and closer to god and the universe; and the most present.
